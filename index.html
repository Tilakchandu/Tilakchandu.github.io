ARTIFACT 01 — AI LAB, WORKSHOP 1

Budget Coach: A Custom GPT for Beginner Budgeting

> objective

To design and build a working AI assistant using OpenAI's Custom GPT builder, applying the design thinking framework (empathy, define, ideate, prototype, test) to a real, narrow problem: helping someone budget for the first time without feeling overwhelmed by it.

> process

Started in the empathy phase by identifying that most first-time budgeters do not fail from a lack of effort, they fail from never learning a simple framework and feeling judged when they try. That led to a strict design rule: the assistant asks about the user's actual struggle before it ever asks for numbers. The first build broke this rule, it jumped straight to income and expenses, so the instructions were rewritten and re-tested until the conversation opened the right way every time. The final version was tested against a full realistic budgeting scenario, including income, fixed expenses, a week of tracked spending, and a follow-up habit change.

> tools

ChatGPT Custom GPT builder · Design Thinking framework · OpenAI usage policy review

> value_proposition

This artifact is evidence that I can take an AI tool from a vague idea to a working, tested product with real boundaries, not just a single clever prompt. For other AI/ML students, it is a concrete example of applying the design thinking process outside of a lecture, including the mistake I had to catch and fix along the way, which is the part most portfolios leave out.

ARTIFACT 02 — GROUP PROJECT, WORKSHOP 2

ML Justification: Real-Time Fraud Detection at Stripe

My contribution to Group 3's Financial Services & Banking industry analysis, a 4-person team project

> objective

Within a 4-person group project analyzing AI in Financial Services and Banking, my assigned role was ML Specialist: identify a real-world company using traditional machine learning for a specific problem, and build a clear, evidence-backed case for why that approach fits.

> process

Researched Stripe's Radar fraud detection system and identified gradient boosted decision trees (XGBoost) as the underlying algorithm. Mapped the engineered features behind the model, device fingerprint, IP-to-billing distance, purchase velocity, and network-wide card history, then built the case for interpretability as the deciding factor over a more opaque deep learning approach. Found and cited a peer-reviewed source supporting tree-based methods on imbalanced fraud data. My two slides were then combined with three teammates' independently researched sections into one 14-slide group deck covering both machine learning and deep learning approaches across the same industry.

> tools

PowerPoint · Stripe engineering documentation · Google Scholar · peer-reviewed research (Nobel et al., 2024)

> value_proposition

This artifact shows a different skill than Artifact 1: working inside a defined role on a team and delivering a scoped, source-backed technical analysis that had to fit logically and visually with three other people's independent work. It demonstrates that I can research a real company's ML approach, justify a technical choice with evidence, and communicate it clearly to an audience that may not have a deep technical background.
